04 — Trend Analytics / Google Trends & Excel

Winter Sports
Interest Analysis

Analyzed and visualized Google Trends data for nine Olympic winter sports to uncover cyclical public interest patterns — identifying event-driven spikes and providing insights to guide content strategy and seasonal marketing timing.

Role Data Analyst
Tools Google Trends, Excel, Data Visualization
Sports Analyzed 9 Olympic Winter Disciplines
Context MSU Milestone Project
01 — The Opportunity

Sports interest is predictable — if you look at the data

For sports media companies, sponsors, and event organizers, one of the most valuable things to know is when public interest in a sport peaks — and how far in advance that interest starts building. Get the timing wrong on a campaign and you're spending money either before anyone cares or after they've moved on.

Google Trends provides a free, continuous signal of public search interest over time. The challenge is knowing how to extract meaningful patterns from the noise — particularly for sports that only have moment-in-time Olympic spikes every four years.

"Google Trends data is free and publicly available. The competitive advantage isn't having the data — it's knowing how to read it well enough to make decisions others won't."

02 — Methodology

Extracting signal from cyclical data

  • 01
    Data Collection via Google Trends Pulled 5-year search interest data for all nine sports: Alpine Skiing, Biathlon, Bobsled, Cross-Country Skiing, Curling, Figure Skating, Ice Hockey, Ski Jumping, and Speed Skating. Exported as CSV and imported into Excel for analysis.
  • 02
    Baseline vs. Peak Calculation Calculated each sport's baseline interest (average interest score during non-Olympic, non-World Championship months) and compared it to peak interest during event windows. This generated a "spike multiplier" showing how dramatically each sport's audience expands during events.
  • 03
    Lead-Time Analysis For each sport, measured how many weeks before an Olympic event search interest started to meaningfully rise above baseline — establishing an "interest runway" for each sport that informs campaign launch timing.
  • 04
    Sport-to-Sport Comparison Built a comparative chart overlaying interest curves for all nine sports across the same time window, making cross-sport comparisons visible at a glance rather than requiring back-and-forth between individual charts.
  • 05
    Seasonal Pattern Identification Separated Olympic years from non-Olympic years to identify which sports maintain consistent seasonal interest regardless of the games vs. which are almost entirely event-dependent.
03 — Key Findings

Not all winter sports behave the same way

9

Sports Analyzed

Across 5 years of Google Trends data — covering two Winter Olympics cycles for comprehensive pattern identification.

18×

Peak Spike — Curling

Curling showed the highest spike multiplier of any sport — nearly zero baseline interest exploding to massive search volume during Olympics, making it the purest event-dependent sport in the dataset.

6 weeks

Average Interest Runway

Across sports, search interest began rising meaningfully about 6 weeks before the Olympic opening ceremony — suggesting campaigns should launch no later than week 7 before the games.

Year-Round

Ice Hockey & Figure Skating

These two sports maintained significant baseline interest in non-Olympic years — driven by NHL seasons and skating competition circuits — making them viable for year-round content strategy.

A key strategic finding: most winter sports are event-dependent, not calendar-dependent. This means campaigns tied to the calendar (e.g., "winter sports season") will underperform compared to campaigns timed to the event cycle — even in non-Olympic years, World Championships and World Cups drive measurable interest spikes.

04 — Strategic Recommendations

Timing is the campaign variable that matters most

  • ↳
    For Olympic years: launch campaigns 6–7 weeks pre-opening ceremony This is when audience interest starts building. Early-launch campaigns can ride the organic interest curve rather than fighting for attention at peak when everyone else is spending.
  • ↳
    For non-Olympic years: anchor content to World Championships and World Cups These events generate measurable (if smaller) interest spikes for most sports. A calendar of these events provides a year-round content framework that doesn't require waiting for the Olympics.
  • ↳
    Treat Ice Hockey and Figure Skating as evergreen — all others as event-driven Only these two sports justify significant off-cycle content investment. For the other seven, concentrate budget around event windows and don't waste spend in the flat baseline periods.
  • ↳
    Use Curling's spike as a cultural moment opportunity Curling's extreme event-dependence means it captures enormous mainstream attention for a short window. Brands with no normal connection to curling can legitimately ride this spike with timely, playful content.
