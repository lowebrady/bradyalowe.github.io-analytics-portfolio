02 — Operational Analytics / Excel

BART Ridership
Data Analysis

Analyzed Bay Area Rapid Transit ridership records to uncover weekday vs. weekend demand patterns, downtown station traffic peaks, and Yellow Line capacity constraints — with data-backed recommendations for service optimization.

Role Data Analyst
Tools Excel, Pivot Tables, Data Visualization
Domain Transportation / Operations
Context MSU Global Technology Experience
01 — The Problem

A transit system with uneven demand

Public transit agencies face a constant balancing act: deploy too few trains and riders experience overcrowding and delays; deploy too many and operating costs spiral. The key to solving this is understanding when and where demand actually concentrates — not guessing.

BART's ridership data offered an opportunity to do exactly that: analyze actual boardings by station, day, and time to find the demand patterns that should drive scheduling and capacity decisions.

"Operational analytics at its core is about resource allocation. Every insight needs to connect back to a decision: run more trains, add staff, change schedules, close a gate."

02 — Analysis Approach

Breaking down ridership by every meaningful dimension

  • 01
    Weekday vs. Weekend Segmentation Split the dataset into weekday and weekend subsets using Excel's WEEKDAY() function, then built separate pivot tables for each. This revealed dramatically different demand profiles that a combined average would have obscured.
  • 02
    Station-Level Traffic Analysis Ranked all stations by total boardings and by peak-hour boardings separately — because the busiest overall station isn't always the most congested during rush hour. Used LARGE() and INDEX/MATCH to surface the top 10 stations on each metric.
  • 03
    Yellow Line Capacity Deep-Dive Isolated all Yellow Line stations and stops, calculated load factors (riders per available seat-mile equivalent) across time windows, and flagged the specific station-hour combinations exceeding capacity thresholds.
  • 04
    Time-of-Day Demand Curves Built hourly ridership charts for weekday vs. weekend at key stations, making the AM/PM peak structure visible — and revealing that some stations had atypically flat demand curves suggesting different trip-purpose mixes.
  • 05
    Service Optimization Modeling Used the demand data to model the ridership impact of two scheduling scenarios: increasing Yellow Line frequency during identified peak windows vs. extending service hours on weekends at downtown stations.
03 — Key Findings

Where the system is stressed — and where it's underused

3×

Weekday vs. Weekend Gap

Peak weekday ridership at downtown stations ran 3× higher than weekend peaks — but weekend demand was far more evenly distributed across hours, suggesting different scheduling logic is needed for each.

Top 5

Stations Drive 40% of Boardings

Five downtown stations accounted for approximately 40% of system-wide boardings — meaning targeted improvements at these hubs have disproportionate system-wide impact.

Yellow

Line Capacity Crunch

Yellow Line stations showed consistent capacity pressure during the 8–9 AM and 5–6 PM windows on weekdays, with load factors well above comfortable thresholds at peak stops.

Off-Peak

Underutilization Opportunity

Mid-day weekday ridership (10 AM–3 PM) ran at roughly 35% of peak capacity system-wide — suggesting room for off-peak promotional fares to shift demand curves.

04 — Recommendations

Data-backed service decisions

  • ↳
    Increase Yellow Line frequency during identified peak windows The capacity data clearly supports adding runs during the 8–9 AM and 5–6 PM windows on weekdays at the flagged stations. This directly addresses the most acute pain point in the system.
  • ↳
    Shift weekend scheduling logic away from peak-hour assumptions Weekend demand is flatter and more distributed. Running trains on a commuter schedule on weekends wastes capacity in the morning and undersupplies it in the midday and evening windows.
  • ↳
    Explore off-peak fare incentives to flatten weekday demand If even 10% of peak-hour riders can be shifted to mid-day through pricing, it materially reduces overcrowding without adding a single train run.
