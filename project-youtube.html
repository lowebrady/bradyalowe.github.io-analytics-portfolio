Role Data Analyst
Tools Excel, Pivot Tables, Data Visualization
Dataset 6,000+ YouTube Trending Videos
Context MSU Global Technology Experience
01 — The Question

What actually makes a YouTube video trend?

YouTube's trending page is one of the most valuable pieces of real estate on the internet — but the factors that drive a video there are poorly understood. Is it views? Likes? Comment velocity? Category? Upload timing? The answer has major implications for content strategy and platform marketing.

This project set out to analyze a dataset of 6,000+ trending videos across categories, regions, and time periods to find which variables most consistently predict trending performance — and translate those findings into recommendations a content team could actually act on.

"With 6,000 rows of data, the challenge isn't collecting insights — it's knowing which questions to ask. Start with the business goal and work backwards to the analysis."

02 — Data & Approach

Cleaning, structuring, then questioning

The raw dataset required significant cleaning before any analysis could begin — inconsistent date formats, blank fields in key columns, and duplicate entries from videos that trended across multiple days all had to be addressed systematically.

  • 01
    Data Cleaning Removed duplicates using Excel's Remove Duplicates function, standardized date fields with TEXT() and DATEVALUE(), and used IFERROR() to handle missing values in engagement columns without corrupting aggregate formulas.
  • 02
    Category Analysis via Pivot Tables Built pivot tables breaking down average views, likes, comment counts, and like-to-dislike ratios by video category. Sorted by median (not mean) to reduce the distortion from outlier mega-viral videos.
  • 03
    Engagement Rate Calculation Created a calculated engagement rate column (likes + comments / views) and used pivot tables to compare engagement across categories — revealing that high-view categories weren't always the highest-engagement ones.
  • 04
    Histograms & Distribution Analysis Built histograms of view counts, trending duration (days on trending page), and upload-to-trending lag time to understand the distribution shapes and identify where most videos cluster.
  • 05
    Summary Tables & Visualization Consolidated findings into executive-style summary tables with conditional formatting to make patterns immediately visible without requiring the reader to interpret raw data.
03 — Key Findings

What the data revealed

6K+

Videos Analyzed

Across multiple categories, upload dates, and trending durations — cleaned and structured before analysis.

Top 3

Categories by Engagement

Entertainment, Music, and How-To & Style consistently outperformed News & Politics and Sports on engagement rate — despite lower raw view counts.

<48h

Median Trending Lag

Most videos reached trending within 48 hours of upload — suggesting that early velocity (first-day views and likes) is the strongest predictor of trending status.

2.3×

Engagement Lift

Videos with comment sections enabled had 2.3× higher engagement rates than those with comments disabled — comments signal community, which the algorithm rewards.

One counterintuitive finding: longer videos didn't perform worse. The dataset showed no significant negative correlation between video length and trending performance — challenging the conventional "keep it short" wisdom for the trending page specifically.

04 — Recommendations

Turning data into content strategy

  • ↳
    Prioritize the first 24 hours Since trending lag is so short, initial upload promotion (email lists, social posts, community posts) has outsized impact. Budget most promotional effort at launch, not day 3.
  • ↳
    Never disable comments The engagement rate lift from enabled comments is significant enough to outweigh moderation concerns for most content types. Use comment pinning and filters instead of disabling entirely.
  • ↳
    Optimize for engagement rate, not raw views Categories like Entertainment trend with fewer absolute views than News because their engagement rates are higher. A smaller but more engaged audience is algorithmically more valuable.
